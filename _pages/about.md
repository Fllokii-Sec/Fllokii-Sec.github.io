For eight years, I coached wrestling, and for much of that time I was also a public middle school teacher. I spent thousands of hours breaking down film, drilling technique until it became instinct, and teaching athletes how to stay calm and think clearly under pressure because in a match, the person who panics loses before the person who makes the mistake. In the classroom, I was doing the same work from a different angle: managing a room full of unpredictable variables, adapting on the fly, and breaking down complex material so it actually stuck. What I didn't realize at the time was that I was training for a second career.

A year ago, I started teaching myself cybersecurity. No shortcuts, no bootcamp handholding just a plan, consistency, and a willingness to grind through the parts that didn't click the first time. That's the same formula I used as when I got thrown into a head coaching position 5 years ago, and tooka team that didnt place in the top 20 at state, to 2nd place in 3 years, and was reconized as coach of the year the last two years.

What I've Been Building

Over the past year I've worked through entry level certifications like Google Proffesional Cybersecurity and Cisco and hands-on platforms including TryHackMe and Hack The Box, tackling everything from network fundamentals to privilege escalation and web application attacks. I've also participated in bug bounty programs, learning to think like an attacker and document findings the way a real engagement demands.

Beyond structured coursework, I run my own home lab to get practice that certifications alone can't give me:


  - Threat detection & defense — I deployed a T-Pot honeypot on Ubuntu Server to capture live attack traffic, and use the ELK stack (Elasticsearch, Logstash,
   Kibana) to analyze and visualize what's hitting it in real time.

  - Soc homelab - I deployed Wazuh (SIEM) integrated with suricata, sysmon, pfsense, and virustotal to monitor logs on a windows agent through a simulated SSH         brute force attack and malware download.

  - Web Application Firewall - I configured an WAF on a vulnerable application and simulated web attacks, then configured the firewall to bloack those same            attacks.


I don't just spin these labs up and walk away — I actively work in them, analyzing samples and traffic the way I'd need to on the job, not just standing up infrastructure and calling it done.

What I Bring to the Table

Coaching and teaching taught me things you can't get from a textbook:


Discipline under pressure — staying composed and methodical when things go wrong is second nature to me.
Critical thinking — breaking down an opponent's tendencies is the same muscle as breaking down an attacker's TTPs.
Coaching & communication — I know how to explain complex technical ideas clearly, whether to a middle schooler, a teammate, a report, or a stakeholder.
Adaptability — managing a classroom of 30 kids with 30 different needs means I've had a lot of practice thinking on my feet and adjusting my approach mid stream.
Relentless work ethic — I always continue to learn, colaborate, and continoulsy hone my craft, because I know there is always something to improve one, and always be taught.


Where I'm Headed

I'm actively pursuing a role as a SOC Analyst or Junior Penetration Tester, with a long-term interest in growing further into offensive security. I'm driven, endlessly curious, and I treat every lab, every CTF, and every finding as one more rep toward being ready for the job on day one — not after I get it.

If you're looking for someone who's self-motivated, coachable, and already doing the work before being asked to, let's talk.
